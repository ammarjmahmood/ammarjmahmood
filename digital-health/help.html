How to Support a Friend

Recognising when someone needs help — and knowing how to respond — can make a life-changing difference. Expand each section for guidance.

Be aware of these behavioural changes in friends or family members:

  • Withdrawing from in-person social activities they previously enjoyed
  • Expressing hopelessness, worthlessness, or persistent sadness
  • Significant changes in sleep patterns — sleeping much more or much less
  • Visible distress after using their phone or checking social media
  • Declining school or work performance alongside increased screen time
  • Talking about feeling bullied, isolated, or excluded online
  • Any talk of self-harm or suicidal thoughts — always take this seriously

Approaching someone about mental health concerns requires care. Useful approaches include:

  • Choose a private, calm setting — not in front of others.
  • Use "I" statements: "I've noticed you seem stressed lately — I care about you."
  • Ask open questions: "How have you been feeling lately?" rather than "Are you okay?"
  • Listen actively. Do not rush to offer solutions — being heard is often the most helpful first step.
  • Avoid dismissing their feelings with phrases like "just delete the app" or "stop being sad."
  • Let them know specific resources exist (this page) and offer to help them access support.
  • Do not promise to keep suicidal thoughts or self-harm secret — safety comes first.
  • Do not shame someone for struggling. Mental health difficulties are not a choice or a weakness.
  • Do not attempt to diagnose or minimise the problem.
  • Do not abandon the conversation if they push back — persistence shows genuine care.
  • Do not wait for a "better time" if someone appears to be in immediate distress.
