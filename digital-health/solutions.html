Individual Solutions

What Individuals Can Do

Governments and corporations have a role to play, but the most immediate and impactful changes come from individual decisions about how, when, and why digital technology is used. These strategies are evidence-based and can start today.

The Core Principle: Intentionality

The most significant shift a person can make is moving from reactive technology use to intentional technology use. Reactive use means picking up the phone out of habit, boredom, or anxiety. Intentional use means using technology for a specific purpose — then stopping.

Research from the MedlinePlus mental health improvement resources and PMC peer-reviewed literature consistently shows that this single mindset shift — independent of any other change — leads to measurably better outcomes for wellbeing, productivity, and relationships.

The 30-Minute Rule

A 2018 study published in the Journal of Social and Clinical Psychology found that limiting social media use to 30 minutes per day led to significant reductions in loneliness and depression over just three weeks — without eliminating social media use entirely.

Eight Strategies That Work

The following strategies are drawn from peer-reviewed research, clinical mental health guidelines, and recommendations from the Canadian Paediatric Society and MedlinePlus.

1

Set Daily Screen Time Limits

Use the built-in Screen Time (iOS) or Digital Wellbeing (Android) settings to set a daily limit for social media apps. Start with 1 hour total. The notification when the limit is reached creates a moment of awareness — the most important first step.

2

Create a Phone-Free Bedroom

Charge devices outside the bedroom overnight. Replace the phone alarm with a dedicated alarm clock. This removes the temptation to scroll before sleep and immediately upon waking — two of the highest-harm usage windows. The Canadian Paediatric Society recommends this for all ages.

3

Curate Your Social Media Feed

Unfollow, mute, or restrict accounts that consistently cause feelings of inadequacy, anxiety, or envy. Follow accounts that educate, inspire, or genuinely entertain. Most platforms allow content preferences to be set without unfollowing a person publicly.

4

Apply the 20-20-20 Rule for Eye Health

Every 20 minutes of screen use, look at something 20 feet away for at least 20 seconds. This resets the focal muscles in the eye, reducing digital eye strain, headaches, and blurred vision — all of which worsen mood and focus.

5

Prioritise Physical Exercise

Exercise is one of the most robustly evidenced treatments for anxiety and depression. Even 20–30 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise three times per week produces measurable improvements in mood, sleep quality, and cognitive function. Screen time often displaces this activity.

6

Turn Off Non-Essential Notifications

Disable all notifications except those requiring immediate action (calls, messages from close contacts). The average smartphone user receives 63 notifications per day. Each one is a deliberate interruption designed to draw attention back to the platform. Eliminating them restores focus.

7

Invest in In-Person Relationships

Research from Harvard's Study of Adult Development found that the single strongest predictor of lifelong mental health and happiness is the quality of close personal relationships — not wealth, fame, or digital presence. Scheduling regular face-to-face time with friends and family is a direct countermeasure to digital isolation.

8

Practice Mindful Consumption

Before opening an app, ask: "What is the specific reason for opening this right now?" If there is no clear answer, put the phone down. Keeping a simple log of how content makes you feel during and after viewing builds self-awareness and reduces passive scrolling over time.

Tools to Help

These built-in and third-party tools make it easier to implement the strategies above.

📱

Screen Time (iOS)

Apple's built-in tool. Set per-app time limits, schedule downtime, and review weekly reports. Found under: Settings → Screen Time.

Free / Built-in
🤖

Digital Wellbeing

Android equivalent. Includes a Focus Mode that temporarily pauses distracting apps and a Wind Down mode for bedtime. Built into most Android devices.

Free / Built-in
🌳

Forest App

A focus timer app where a virtual tree grows while the phone is unused. Leaving the app kills the tree. Coins earned plant real trees worldwide.

Low Cost
🧘

Headspace / Calm

Guided meditation and mindfulness apps. Even 10 minutes per day of meditation has been shown to reduce anxiety and improve focus in as little as two weeks.

Freemium

A Sample Healthy Digital Routine

Small, consistent changes to daily habits can produce significant mental health improvements within weeks.

🌅
Morning

No phone for the first 30 minutes after waking. Use this time for breakfast, stretching, or journalling. Checking notifications immediately after waking puts the brain into a reactive state that can persist for hours.

☀️
Daytime

Use devices purposefully. Apply the 20-20-20 rule during screen sessions. Keep social media to defined time slots (e.g., 15 minutes at lunch) rather than open-ended browsing. Turn off non-essential notifications.

🌆
Evening

Prioritise in-person connection. Meals without phones strengthen relationships and allow genuine conversation. Schedule one screen-free activity per evening — a walk, reading, board games, or creative hobbies.

🌙
Bedtime

Screens off at least 1 hour before sleep. Charge the phone outside the bedroom. Replace pre-sleep scrolling with reading (physical book), light stretching, or breathing exercises. This one change typically improves sleep quality within 3–5 days.

Struggling? Real Support Is Available

If digital habits have already significantly impacted mental health, individual strategies may not be enough on their own. Trained counsellors and crisis support workers are ready to help — for free, 24/7.
